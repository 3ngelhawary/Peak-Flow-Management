Retention Tank for Storm Pump Station Peak Flow Management

A retention tank temporarily stores excess stormwater when incoming flow exceeds pump station capacity, then releases the stored volume gradually after the storm peak.

Peak Flow Relief Flood Risk Reduction Pump Station Protection Controlled Return Flow

Problem Statement

Existing storm pump stations may become hydraulically overloaded during intense storm events. When the incoming stormwater flow exceeds available pumping capacity, the system may experience surcharge, surface flooding, and operational stress.

Qin > Qpump

Under this condition, excess flow accumulates upstream of the pump station. This can increase hydraulic grade line levels, overload manholes, reduce freeboard, and create risk to electrical, mechanical, and downstream force main assets.

Design Objective

☔

Store Peak Excess

Capture the temporary flow volume above pump station capacity during the storm peak.

↘

Release Gradually

Return stored water to the system after the peak using controlled gravity or pumped discharge.

🛡

Protect Assets

Reduce surcharge risk and protect the pump station, wet well, controls, and downstream force main.

Concept Layout Diagram

The diversion chamber routes normal flow to the pump station and diverts excess peak flow into the retention tank. Stored water is returned under controlled conditions after the peak event.

Hydraulic Design Section

Storage Equation

V = ∫(Qin - Qout) dt

Required storage is calculated from the cumulative difference between incoming storm flow and allowable outgoing flow during the critical storm duration.

Required Inputs

  • Rainfall return period
  • Inflow hydrograph
  • Pump station capacity
  • Tank emptying time
  • Operational safety factor

Design Procedure Timeline

Use the steps below as a practical engineering workflow for concept-to-commissioning delivery.

Collect as-built drawings, pump curves, catchment data, rainfall records, utility records, and site constraints.
Build or update the stormwater model to define peak inflow hydrographs and surcharge locations.
Calculate required storage volume using the excess flow hydrograph and selected emptying strategy.
Select the preferred storage system based on land, constructability, maintenance, cost, and groundwater conditions.
Check soil bearing, excavation support, buoyancy, cover loading, traffic loading, and groundwater uplift.
Define return pumps, instruments, power supply, control philosophy, alarms, and operation sequence.
Construct, test, clean, commission controls, verify return operation, and hand over O&M documentation.

Engineering Checks

HGL / Backwater

Confirm upstream levels remain within allowable limits.

Buoyancy / Uplift

Check empty tank stability under groundwater conditions.

Groundwater

Assess dewatering, waterproofing, and excavation risks.

Sedimentation

Provide slopes, sumps, flushing, and cleaning access.

Maintenance Access

Confirm safe entry, ventilation, lifting, and cleaning routes.

Emergency Overflow

Provide safe overflow path for events above design capacity.

Downstream Capacity

Verify controlled return does not overload force main or outfall.

Tank Type Comparison

Underground Concrete Tank

Pros

  • Durable
  • High structural capacity
  • Suitable below roads

Cons

  • Higher cost
  • Longer construction
  • Needs uplift checks

Modular HDPE Tank

Pros

  • Fast installation
  • Lightweight
  • Flexible layout

Cons

  • Needs careful bedding
  • Limited traffic depth
  • Inspection access required

Open Basin

Pros

  • Easy inspection
  • Lower structural cost
  • Simple overflow

Cons

  • Requires land
  • Safety fencing needed
  • Odor and mosquito control

Inline Storage Pipe / Tunnel

Pros

  • Uses pipeline corridor
  • Compact footprint
  • Good for urban areas

Cons

  • Limited volume
  • Cleaning challenges
  • Hydraulic control critical
